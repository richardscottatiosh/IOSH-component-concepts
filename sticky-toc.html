Hierarchy of controls

A systematic approach to minimising or eliminating exposure to hazards in occupational health and safety. Controls are ranked from most to least effective.

Elimination

The most effective control measure is to eliminate the hazard entirely from the workplace. This removes all risk at source and is always the preferred approach where practicable.

Substitution

Where elimination is not possible, substituting the hazard with something less dangerous can significantly reduce risk. Replacing a toxic solvent with a less harmful alternative is a common example.

Engineering controls

Physical changes to the work environment that reduce exposure to hazards — guarding machinery, installing local exhaust ventilation, or using interlocks to prevent unsafe operation.

Administrative controls

Changes to how work is done: safe work procedures, job rotation, restricted access arrangements, and training programmes that limit workers' exposure to hazards.

Personal protective equipment

The last resort when other controls are not sufficient on their own. PPE includes gloves, safety glasses, hearing protection, and respiratory equipment.
